Today I had to replace the low voltage battery in my 2020 Tesla Model 3. Outside of tires, wiper blades, and air filters, this is the first maintenance I've had to do.

Let that sink in for a second. Five years of ownership, and the first actual repair was a $139 battery swap done in my own driveway.

## Yes, a Tesla has a regular car battery

This surprises people every time it comes up. Underneath all the electric drivetrain magic, my 2020 Model 3 has a plain old 12V lead-acid battery, the same basic technology that's been starting gas cars since forever.

It doesn't turn over an engine, obviously. Instead it powers all the low voltage stuff: the computers, the screen, the door handles, the lights. It's also what wakes the car up, because the big high voltage pack stays disconnected behind contactors until the car needs it. If the 12V battery dies completely, the car can't close those contactors, and you've got a very expensive brick with flush door handles.

Newer Model 3s switched to a 16V lithium-ion low voltage battery that lasts much longer, but 2020 cars like mine got the lead-acid version. Typical lifespan on those is somewhere in the 3 to 5 year range, so mine actually held on longer than I had any right to expect.

## The car tells you before it dies

This is the part I genuinely appreciate. With a gas car, the classic 12V battery failure mode is walking out to a car that won't start, usually in a parking lot, usually when you're already late.

Tesla monitors the battery constantly. When mine started to go, I got a warning on the touchscreen and in the app telling me the low voltage battery needed replacement soon. That's the screenshot at the top of this post. No drama, no being stranded, just "hey, handle this in the next few weeks."

The car kept driving completely normally in the meantime. I got to schedule the fix on my terms instead of the battery's.

## Mobile service for $139: why I didn't bother with DIY

I had two real options here, and I'm comfortable with a socket set, so DIY was genuinely on the table. The battery lives under the frunk, and the job is mostly trim panel, first responder loop, terminals, hold-down bracket, swap. Squarely in weekend-wrencher territory.

Then I priced out Tesla Mobile Service and stopped considering it. The total came to $139: $100 for the battery and $39 for labor. For thirty-nine dollars, a tech drives to my house and does the job in my driveway while I keep working.

That's barely more than what a parts store would charge me for a comparable battery alone, minus the part where I source the right one, watch the disconnect procedure video twice, and fight the plastic trim clips. I was busy that week, so I booked it in the app and paid the price. No dealer visit, no waiting room, no loaner logistics.

This is the part of Tesla ownership that doesn't get enough attention. The cars have real flaws, but the service model of "a van comes to you and the labor costs less than a pizza" is something I wish every manufacturer would copy.

## Five years of maintenance, itemized

Here's the complete maintenance history of this car since 2020:

- Tires (worn like any car, arguably a bit faster thanks to instant torque)
- Wiper blades
- Cabin air filters
- One 12V battery

That's it. No oil changes, no transmission service, no spark plugs, no belts, no exhaust work, no brake jobs. Regenerative braking does most of the stopping, so my brake pads have barely worn.

I haven't added up every receipt, but compare that list to five years of a gas car: 15 or so oil changes at minimum, plus whatever else comes up. The gap isn't subtle. The maintenance savings alone cover a meaningful chunk of the EV price premium, and that's before you touch fuel costs.

## Takeaways

- EVs still have a 12V battery, and it's the most likely thing to fail on you. Budget for one every 3 to 5 years if you have a lead-acid car.
- The early warning system works. Don't ignore it, but don't panic either.
- Five years and the first real repair cost $139 and zero minutes of my time. I'm not going back to oil changes.

If you've got an early Model 3 and haven't replaced your 12V yet, your warning is probably coming. When it does, you now know it's nothing to stress about.
